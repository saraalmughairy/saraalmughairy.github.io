steadypack

An intelligent device to monitor healthy backpack use.

context

MHCID course: Prototyping Studio

timeline

March 2016 (9 days)

project scope

3 types of prototyping

teammates

Ankit Potdar and Matthew Eziashi.

Video prototype, user testing, poster & logo design

01.

project context



an exercise in rapid prototyping

Through triangulation of three prototyping techniques: video, model, and arduino, we were able to evaluate the desirability, usability, and feasibility of SteadyPack.

02.

problem space



how can we help adults and children practice better backpack health?

Improper backpack use can lead to health problems, such as back, neck, shoulder strain, and headaches. This is especially common and problematic in children, who often far exceed the recommended weight limit of 15-20% of body weight and are in the process of developing postural muscles.

03.

solution



a clip-on backpack accessory that promotes healthy backpack-wearing

by informing the user when the backpack is over the recommended weight, how to achieve better weight distribution while loading their backpack, whether weight is equally distributed across their shoulders, and whether they have correct posture while wearing the backpack.

04.

prototype: concept



research question: is SteadyPack desirable?

Using an unfinished model prototype, we filmed the product and its essential functions and selling points, using visual effects as needed to demonstrate planned features. We tailored the script and editing to the format of a standard crowdfunding pitch or product marketing video, to reflect the sort of contextual environment that users would expect to see a product like this in.




To find out what people thought of our concept, we had 23 people view our video and respond to a survey. In this survey, we found that most respondents (82%) sympathized with the problem, attributing shoulder and back pain to improper backpack use. 95% of respondents felt that the SteadyPack may be able to reduce the problem, but only 27% would definitely use it for themselves, mostly because of lack of regular concern for the problem (50%). Respondents also expressed reluctance to install multiple parts, or use a visible backpack accessory.




05.

prototype: feasibility



Research Question: is it technically feasible to make SteadyPack?

Our motivation for creating an arduino prototype was twofold: we wanted to evaluate the technical feasibility of providing real-time meaningful information about backpack use and to provide us with a means for user-testing (otherwise we would have had to do a Wizard Of Oz user test).




For the scope of this project, we decided to implement two of SteadyPack’s four features using Arduino: informing the user if (1) based on their bodyweight, if their backpack weighs more than the recommended maximum weight and (2) if the weight was unequally distributed across their shoulders. Based on secondary research and sketching out schematics, we predicted that the way to achieve this would be to use pressure sensors under the bag’s straps (to collect info on weight distribution between the shoulders) and under the bag's handle (to operate similarly to a hanging scale).





We were unsure ahead of time if we should go with the larger sensors, which would provide less computational noise but risk bending since they would be sandwiched between the user’s straps and shoulders or the smaller sensors, which would would be less likely to bend, but could be less accurate due to spanning less of the contact between the users shoulder’s and straps, so we purchased and tested both. In our early sketches, we imagined using rubber padding in between the sensor and the strap, to better distribute weight over the sensor, reducing noise. We ended up being able to use a couple of layers of electrical tape to achieve the same thing. Even with this padding, there was still too much noise when we tested it, so through our arduino code, we threw out any sensor data that was more that two standard deviations away from the average.

06.

prototype: behavioral



Research Questions: Is the clip-on usable? What mode of feedback do users understand best?

When coming up with the form factor of the model, we decided that we wanted it to self-contained to the backpack (ie., not require a separate smartphone application and computer interface) because we wanted it be useful to a variety of people (e.g., children) and for a variety of activities (e.g., backpacking). We decided the feedback provided should be accessible to both the wearer themselves and others so that in the case of children, caretakers are able to perceive the feedback and assist the children accordingly, meaning it should have some sort of external, visual display. Lastly, we wanted it to be an accessory, not a smart backpack, so that users could retrofit whatever bag they already owned or preferred.





Using a laser cutter, acrylic, LED lights, and Velcro, we crafted a clip on accessory that provided feedback using various light patterns. We had to experiment with several patterns of particle board cutting to find a consistency that was flexible enough to fold over the backpack strap without snapping in half, but sturdy enough to hold the display. We wanted to know if users would find the shape and function of the clip it intuitive , so we asked users where they assume the SteadyPack would go. 5 out of 5 users tested answered that they would clip it around the strap of the backpack. Users found the lighting interface intuitive, although comprehension was varied slightly. Most users understood the general idea of what the lights indicated, but each user phrased it differenty. Haptic feedback was unanimously preffered when users were asked if they would favor vibrations instead of lights. One user mentioned that lights may be more desirable for children, but preferred vibrations over lights for herself. 2 parents we interviewed said that while they thought it was a concern, rather than helping their child carry less weight, they thought this would be a good tool to teach their child to carry weight (even excess weight) properly. One of the parents responded by supplying an image showing just how large the volume of supplies heir child is expected to carry is (thereby relating to the magnitude of the problem). Lastly, multiple participants mentioned ideas for specialized use cases, as opposed to every day use: military personnel often carry a large volume of weight, professional athletes need to avoid injury at all costs, backpackers and street bikers both care a ton about exact weight and weight distribution. Overall, users testing the model prototype found the problem relatable and the solution intuitive, but the use of lights on the device less than ideal.
