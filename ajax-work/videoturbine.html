Problem Space.


Video has become increasingly important for businesses.

Videos are being used more than ever before for advertising products on the internet. Consumers are looking to YouTube, Facebook, and eCommerce sites to find product videos that will assist in their online purchase decisions.


Yet having videos made is a pain.

Despite the increasing volume of videos needed, getting a commercial product video produced has yet to be streamlined. The chart below depicts the findings from a survey of US ad buyers who cited the following challenges in buying commercial video (% of respondents, eMarketer 2015).




Video production is associated with high cost, specialized labor, technical jargon and opaque creativity, which makes the user experience difficult for clients and producers.


For Clients:

Budgets for video projects range drastically, from hundreds of dollars to millions, with sometimes seemingly little difference between end results. This leaves clients feeling uncertain about the value of money spent on video services, and wary of being taken advantage of.


For Producers:

Because clients are often cautious or unfamiliar with the video production process, producers bear the responsibility of proving their worth- explaining and justifying costs, and reassuring clients of shared expectations. Producers are worried that clients will be unsatisfied with work produced, and are similarly wary of being exploited or cheated.


Overall, this lack of mutual trust and understanding results in friction and reduced efficiency throughout the entire process for both parties.

Opportunity.


How can we make video production more accessible to businesses?

A survey of 15 video production company websites revealed strong similarities in process descriptions. A subject matter expert interview confirmed this process description and added qualitative insights regarding workload and client concerns during each step. This chart depicts a typical video production process, as described by this research.


Video production companies treat each video project as a unique idea, with a creative process that can last for weeks and cost anywhere from a few thousand dollars to several million. With such a massive volume of commercial videos being produced every day, what is the likelihood that each individual video actually benefits from being treated as unique? While certain videos may require a full creative process (e.g., comedic or cinematic tv commercials), many videos designed for eCommerce and social media ("Insider" style product demos, "Tasty" style aerial food recipe videos) are strikingly similar in style and content, presenting the opportunity for a streamlined approach.

Solution.

VideoTurbine.com
A Web-Based Video Production Service that Enables Businesses to Design and Track their Product Videos.

Video Turbine provides a structured format, similar to ordering other personalized items through eCommerce, where businesses can choose from a selection of tailored options to generate a recipe for their video. They then ship their product to Video Turbine and track video production and editing through the online interface. Within a few days, their video is ready to share.


System Design.

A simplified model of our system shows orders placed by clients using the Video Turbine Website. Our team then recieves details of these orders as instructions, and schedules production activities in a way that maximizes efficiency (e.g, all videos the require a white backdrop are shot at the same time). Meanwhile, the client is informed of shoot progress via the video tracker.

User Flow for ordering an eCommerce Product Video.

The user begins by using our Video Builder tool to select a product video template (full actor demo, hand demo, or 360 spin). They are then provided with options based on that template (e.g., backdrop color). By having full control over these options and completely transparent pricing, they are able to feel confident about the details of their video prior to submitting the order. Once they submit the order, they simply print out a shipping label and send us their product. During this waiting period, they are never in the dark- the online order tracker updates them at each step of the production process. Once the video is complete, they have the option to request one revision before receiving a download link to their video in web and social-media-ready formats, as well as all raw footage.

Research.

Testing Desirability and Usability of Video Turbine

Background

We created a prototype of Video Turbine that is a live, fully functioning WooCommerce website.

This decision was a tradeoff between control over visual design and functionality, but we ultimately decided while typically, mockups can test the usability and desirability of a product, that the increased functionality and authenticity of a real eCommerce site would help us better assess whether businesses would actually be willing to 'shop' for their video online, the same way they would use an eCommerce site to buy other products. Additionally, to assess desirability, we created an explainer video (see above) for the service which we intend to send around to business owners along with a questionaire. Lastly, throughout the website, we are collecting constant feedback through a Suggestion Box form and a Desired Features poll.



Research Question:

How can we make Video Turbine more usable?

To find ways to improve the usability of interactions on Video Turbine, we sat down with 3 of our friends and had them build a video while thinking aloud and explaining what they thought each step and feature was. They were instructed to 'build a video that looks like this' (we showed them an example video that was not created by Video Turbine). This simple exercise gave us extremely valuable insights both about micro-interactions on the site and copy improvements, but also on desired features. Testing Feasibility of Pre-Production via an Interface.

Background:

Cognitive and market research show that people tend to be happier, less anxious, and more confident making choices when there are fewer options (known as the Paradox of Choice).

There is a reason why Squarespace is more user friendly than Wix: Squarespace provides choice within limitation, whereas Wix promotes customizing every pixel. How many of us have spent hours designing our own sneakers on NikeID, only to never actually purchase them, opting instead for their predesigned shoes despite the price similarity? After our collaborative design exercise, we felt that using an interface to 'shop' video features could take the place of an in-person consultation, and that the added transparency could increase users' confidence about what their resulting video would look like. Of course, we were wary of overwhelming users by requiring them to choose every little detail about the video: We wanted users to have just enough control to feel confident about what they were ordering, without burdening them with too much choice.

Research Question:

Can we produce a product video that matches the quality and style of what the user had in mind, using only the information collected through our interface?

So, we sought out to test what was the abolute minimal information a video producer needed in order to create the product video the user had in mind. Despite Evan's lengthy consulation meetings and emails with clients as a freelancer, he felt that he only required the following information from them: whether they wanted a solid-color backdrop or studio location, whether there would be actors involved, and what specific features of the product should be demonstrated. I found a video of a product demo online and attempted to have Evan recreate it without ever watching it. I only provided him with the kinds of details easily attained from an interface (Backdrop color, Voiceover or Actor Speaking on Camera, Captions or No Captions, Duration, and Script). The resulting video was, in our opinion, comparable in style and content, and equivalent or better in quality. We are in the process of conducting a survey on Amazon Mechanical Turk, to find out from an unbiased audience which of the two videos demonstrates higher quality, which video they prefer, and which video better represented the product. I will post the results when this survey is complete.

By having Evan reproduce this video using only the simple instructions I provided, we verified that it is indeed feasible to produce high quality product videos with limited instructions communicated solely through an interface. We also realized that we either need to limit the types of products we create videos for, or provide a few more options that are product-specific. For instance, in the video Evan reproduced for this exercise, the original actor wears the product (boots) for a few seconds on camera. If we were to offer apparel videos, we would need to either enable users to specify whether the apparel should be worn and how (e.g., the original actor had pants that covered the upper portion of the boots), or alternatively, make it clear that the apparel will not be showcased on an actual person. Lastly, in this exercise, I provided a verbatim copy of the original script, which many of Evan's regular clients do, but we hypothesized that we could reduce the workload of many clients by having them instead provide a list of product features, and have us generate a draft of a script for them, along with a corresponding shot list that makes it clear what visual will be displayed during each line of the script (e.g., when the script says "This shoe has a flexible sole", a corresponding note might say "Actor uses both hands to slightly bend shoe").
