01.

problem space


45% of women in the US reported feeling unsafe walking alone at night

GALLUP 2016

While women are actually less likely to be the victims of street crime than men, they tend to be more afraid of it, which is often referred to as "the fear of crime paradox" (Pryor, 2013). For the scope of this project, we chose to exclusively focus on women because the nature of women’s fear tends to differ from men’s fear in both its cause and effect: women tend to be afraid of sexual violence and sexual harassment in particular, leading them to develop specific preventative strategies of distancing themselves in both space and time from potential attackers. Importantly, fear of walking is hypothesized to further restrict the freedoms of women (Pryor et al. 2013). Women’s mobility is restricted by the perception of certain spaces as masculine, a product of women’s historical exclusion from these spaces, and by the harassment and violence they face in some spaces. Notably, what this suggests is that walking inequality perpetuates itself, that women's fear may be both a result and cause of women’s inequality (Koskela, 2000).

02.

opportunity


How can we help women be and feel safer when walking alone?

Walking alone is for many an everyday experience and though physical assault is relatively rare, fear and uneasiness is all too common and felt regardless, influencing walking behavior and attitudes. There are many products that focus on seeking help in the midst of an attack, but none that holistically target the walking experience. Further, due to fear being a function of a larger societal issue, we also chose to consider solutions beyond the context of the immediate walk.

high-intensity experience
everyday experience

After a literature review, interviews with subject matter experts, and two months of primary user research including field research, diary studies, and interviews, we uncovered three interrelated elements of the current experience that we wanted our solution to address.

1.

How can we facilitate easier communication of fear - both during and beyond the context of the immediate walk?


2.

How can we enable our walkers to impact their surrounding environment?


3.

How can we help our walkers effectively assess and respond to risks?
