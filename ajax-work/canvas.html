01.

problem space



context

Canvas is an learning management platform created for managing course work between students and instructors. Our task was to examine the existing platform to find design gaps and present a redesign.


considerations

In addition to the assignment prompt, I added the following constraints to my redesign: Canvas, from a business perspective, needs the design solution to fit well with the rest of their system to minimize cost of redesign of other elements and to avoid needing to re-train people who are already familiar with using their existing system. Similarly, while I chose to focus on the student-side of Canvas, I took steps to be mindful of how design modifications could influence the workflow of instructors.

02.

opportunity



1. Increase engagement in discussion forums.

Preliminary research surfaced a lack of deep engagement between students and Canvas. The 2014 Learning Management Service (LMS) Survey surfaced that only 41% of students felt that they were “engaging in meaningful interactions with other students” through Canvas. Semistructured interviews revealed that Discussion forums, an area that should be particularly rich with engagement, was noticeably bleak.


2. Improve information architecture.

In the 2014 LMS Survey open response section, 17 respondents complained of difficulty in finding or navigating information, exposing the poor information hierarchy of Canvas. Interestingly, all 11 of my Photo Survey participants reported heavily relying on the Canvas calendar feature for most navigation.


3. Enable post-assignment reflection.

Within my cohort, I noticed that students are often interested in viewing each other’s submissions, especially for creative assignments, an opportunity that Canvas is uniquely positioned to support.


03.

solution



feature 1

Increase engagement in discussion forums by allowing students to collaboratively annotate readings.


feature 2

Improve information architecture by utilizing the dashboard real estate, making it quicker to view upcoming deadlines and navigate to course materials through a single click.


feature 3

Enable post-assignment reflection, by allowing students to publish uploads for others to view and “follow” each other’s work.

sketches

Potential designs for time tracking concepts. A circular timer could function as a manual punch clock, logging time into the assignment, which the user can then choose to submit anonymously alongside their work, to let instructors know how much time the assignment really took. A chart shows the student how much time they spent on comparable assignments in the past.

A reimaginination of the discussion forum concept, where students annotate assigned readings directly and collaboratively, rather than posting their individual responses to a forum thread. Tools like highlighting, hyperlinks, and reaction emojis could enable students to interact directly with the material, offering a more engaging experience.

One layout for a news feed page, where students see assignments submitted by their peers for feedback and commentary. In this instance, students like and comment on an infographic submitted by one of their peers. Scrolling down would reveal other sumbissions. In the lefthand column, the student selects which courses and individuals they want to see in their feed.

usability testing

Try out the interactive mock up I used for usability testing.

Three UW students were tested one-on-one at a coffee shop using the interactive prototype linked above. While testing, I took notes on printed copies of the wireframes. Additionally, I recorded participants mouse movements and clicks using the Adobe XD screen recording feature. The screener and script used can be found in my process book . The goal of this study was to assess: (1) Is the redesigned Discussion feature usable and desirable?; (2) Could the same annotation tools added to the Discussion have added benefit for other parts of Canvas (e.g., online peer critiques, instructor grading)?; (3) Is the layout of the new dashboard on the home page intuitive?; (4) Do students like the idea of being able to “follow” other students whose work they like?

05.

visual design



style matrix

Comparison of visual styles of project management/ scheduling softwares, by tone and text usage. For the Canvas redesign, I chose to go with a more causal, text-reduced visual experience, to take out some of the formality and dryness Canvas currently has.

color

To develop the color palette, I started with the University of Washington’s official colors, and used the signature purple to create analogous and complementary color harmonies. I also considered the colors used by Slack, which I identified as a good model for emulation. I compared these palettes and selected colors from each, to create a color palette that conveyed a strong sense of UW branding, but with cool, bright accents to reinforce clickability and establish clear visual hierarchy.


visual language

I aimed to preserve the professionalism and simplicity of the original canvas design, albeit with a more energetic tone, so I kept the clean, rectangular structure of the wireframes, eliminating extraneous details wherever possible and relying on the highlight colors (blue and green) to indicate key information and clickable links/buttons.
