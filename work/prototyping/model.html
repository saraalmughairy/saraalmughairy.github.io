Immersion Blender

For this prototyping exercise, I explored the design of Oxo’s hand-held immersion blender.

Before creating my three prototypes, I read over the OXO immersion blender product description, watched a few videos of people using immersion blenders online, and then finally, sketched out a handful of ides for prototypes. I narrowed down my ideas to three main directions, and then used a combination of foam, air-dry clay, tape, and dowels, and sink stoppers purchased from the 99 Cent store to bring my prototypes to fruition.

Prototype 1

The user had trouble pressing the correct control on the grip because they couldn’t see which button was which. If they used it frequently, this design might be preferable because they could simply memorize the location of the three buttons and be able to use them using the same hand they are holding the device with, but otherwise, it is too difficult to figure out the location of each button. Additionally, given the way the device is gripped, the display should be rotated 90 degrees, though this could prove problematic for accommodating left and right-handed users.

Prototype 2

This was the prototype the user preferred the most. The user found it easy to hold and manipulate and easy to switch speed settings while using it. The user pointed out that the change in dimension of the cone shape should be less extreme: that nobody’s hand is as small as the cone is near the bottom, so there is more room to create a more nuanced slope in dimension.

Prototype 3

This prototype did not test well because they was not enough resistance in the spring mechanism (the two handle prongs move together too easily). When the user held it, he wanted to squeeze the two prongs together just to be able to hold the device steadily, not to increase the speed. Otherwise, the placement of things felt intuitive to the user.
