Concept Prototyping

Create a video “prototype” for an existing transportation product.

I selected Seattle bike-sharing service Pronto as the subject for my video, because I thought it had the most potential for live-action visual demonstration. To show the motivation, usage, and functionality, I developed a narrative about a young professional, new to Seattle, struggling to find a fast and reliable commuting method. Because Pronto’s target user is unlikely to be a cyclist already, I wanted to keep the focus of the video on Pronto in comparison to traditional methods of commuting, not as an alternative to bicycles in general. And because Pronto primarily operates in an area with an especially high percentage of young adults and transplants, I opted for a lighthearted and comedic tone tailored for this demographic. Although this chosen tone lends itself to hyperbolic details for comic effect (being so late to work that the workday is over, losing ten jobs in two weeks, etc.), the visual story transcends the comedic elements and communicates the essential pitch of Pronto: user-friendly bike sharing as a fast, simple option for personal transportation.

Process

Pre-Production

After iterating on the narrative structure and deliberating the most engaging way to communicate the motivation for using Pronto, I wrote a voiceover-driven script that uses the power of creative storytelling to hold the viewer’s attention while conveying the fundamental Pronto experience and its main benefits: speed and ease of use. Reverse-engineering a script to match these benefits, I created a scenario where the main character’s problem is that his transportation options are all too slow or too difficult. He is perpetually late and visibly unhappy. After a brief but substantial exposition of this character and his needs, I presented Pronto as the solution, showing through live demonstration how easy it is to use, and how much faster the character can move with it. As far as digital interface is concerned, I limited the focus to the station kiosk, where the majority of user interaction with Pronto occurs. Although they do have a website and an app (though I can’t find it in the App store), these are largely supplementary and extraneous to the essential user experience. Usage occurs primarily in person at the station. After showing and discussing how the product is used, I ended the script with a light and happy conclusion to the story: now that he uses Pronto, the main character is visibly happier and more punctual.

Production

When watching Pronto’s existing demonstration videos, I found them lacking in style. They felt corporate and too information-driven to engage me as a viewer. I admired the Nest Protect video that we watched in class, and identified cinematic composition as one of many reasons that it drew me in. So for the visual design of my video, I used a combination of camera and composition techniques to achieve a cohesive “look” that would make the video feel filmic, complimenting the use of character-driven narrative to immerse the viewer in a cinematic world. For example, I used wide shots to establish scenes before cutting to close-ups for detail, emulating film grammar. I also used close-up reaction shots to show the character’s emotional response to events, and used shallow focus to guide the viewer’s eye to the subject of the frame. Planning carefully, I made use of high frame rates when shooting, to be slowed down in post. While this effect is easier to achieve than it looks, it did require deliberate strategizing prior to shooting. In addition to the storyboarded shots, supplementary shots and angles were improvised on location. The diversity of environments in the University District made suitable locations for these shots easy to find, so in most cases, the resulting shot is fairly similar to the original storyboarded concept. My two volunteer actors were great to work with, and performed well with minimal direction. The editing was tedious, but I got ample coverage and the shots lined up as expected.

Reflection

It turned out great!

Overall, I am very satisfied with the result of the process. The fun, comedic tone may not be suited for every application, but I can see this video being used for a lighthearted pitch, or for online marketing of the service. In my opinion, the limitations of my approach are 1) that it took several days of shooting and editing, and is therefore not really feasible as a draft-quality mockup, and 2) that it uses an information-light script that spends time developing character and narrative, instead of cutting right to the chase. Conversely, what works best about it is that it conveys value to potential users in a visual, comprehensible, and entertaining way. Ultimately, I am confident that, despite the hit-or-miss risk, this video holds up as an informational tool to convey the value of the product in a way that viewers will actually remember.

Pront Cycle Share ended up using this video for paid advertisements!
