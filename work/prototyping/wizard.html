Wizard of Oz

Evaluate the utility and usability of a navigational tool for assisting visual impairment.

Currently, the vision-impaired community utilizes accessible navigation tools such as google maps to traverse new areas with high-level, spoken directions. These simple left and right commands are helpful at the street level. However, GPS mapping quickly breaks down in smaller, indoor spaces. Navigating around the chairs and tables in a room requires a more precise coordinate mapping than GPS technologies provide. Certain buildings may also block GPS signals all together. After a brief interview with our Subject Matter Expert, Cynthia Bennett, we discovered that an indoor navigation aid serves a real need for the vision-impaired. Cindy explained several situations in which she has struggled to return to a speci c indoor location. For example, when attending a concert at the Seattle symphony, she needed to return to her seat after being delayed by long lines at intermission. Unfortunately, she became disoriented as to the location of her seat and had to nd an usher to guide her. She felt embarrassed about both walking in late and needing help getting to her seat. Here we saw an opportunity for a device that can be used as a type of locator beacon indoors. Ideally, a vision-impaired user would be able to mark their starting location, navigate away from the space, and then access step-by-step directions when it was time to make a return trip. Alternatively, if a vision-impaired user were meeting a friend at a new location, their friend would be able to mark their own path, share it, and allow the vision-impaired user to access detailed directions upon entry to the building.

Reflection

In some senses, this prototyping method is limited (i.e. transitions are limited, less customizable and interactive than coding), but it is quite capable for demonstrating navigation and interactions, which was the goal of this particular process.
