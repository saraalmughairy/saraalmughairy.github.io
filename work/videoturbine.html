Problem Space.

Video has become increasingly important for businesses —

Videos are being used more than ever before for advertising products on the internet. Consumers are looking to YouTube, Facebook, and eCommerce sites to find product videos that will assist in their online purchase decisions.


Yet having videos made is a pain —

Despite the increasing volume of videos needed, getting a commercial product video produced has yet to be streamlined. The chart below depicts the findings from a survey of US ad buyers who cited the following challenges in buying commercial video (% of respondents, eMarketer 2015).




Video production is associated with high cost, specialized labor, technical jargon and opaque creativity, which makes the user experience difficult for clients and producers.


For Clients —

Budgets for video projects range drastically, from hundreds of dollars to millions, with sometimes seemingly little difference between end results. This leaves clients feeling uncertain about the value of money spent on video services, and wary of being taken advantage of.


For Producers —

Because clients are often cautious or unfamiliar with the video production process, producers bear the responsibility of proving their worth- explaining and justifying costs, and reassuring clients of shared expectations. Producers are worried that clients will be unsatisfied with work produced, and are similarly wary of being exploited or cheated.


Overall, this lack of mutual trust and understanding results in friction and reduced efficiency throughout the entire process for both parties.

Opportunity.

How can we make Product Video Production more accessible?

A survey of 15 video production company websites revealed strong similarities in process descriptions. A subject matter expert interview confirmed this process description and added qualitative insights regarding workload and client concerns during each step. A contextual inquiry of EvanScottMoore.com's video production process surfaced extreme inefficiency in the pre-production and post-production communication process. The vast majority of the time is spent on client-producer communication, not creating videos. Clients have the same questions over and over, resulting in on average 30 emails per step, which is difficult to keep track of and time consuming. Also surfaced in the competitive analysis, is that video production companies treat each video project as a unique idea, with a creative process that can last for weeks and cost anywhere from a few thousand dollars to several million. While certain videos may require a full creative process (e.g., comedic or cinematic tv commercials), many videos designed for eCommerce and social media ("Insider" style product demos, "Tasty" style aerial food recipe videos) are strikingly similar in style and content, presenting the opportunity for a streamlined approach.

Solution.

VideoTurbine.com
A Web-Based Video Production Service that Enables Businesses to Design and Track their Product Videos.

Video Turbine provides a structured format, similar to ordering other personalized items through eCommerce, where businesses can choose from a selection of tailored options to generate a recipe for their video. They then ship their product to Video Turbine and track video production and editing through the online interface. Within a few days, their video is ready to share.

User Flow —

The user begins by using our Video Builder tool to select a product video template (full actor demo, hand demo, or 360 spin). They are then provided with options based on that template (e.g., backdrop color). By having full control over these options and completely transparent pricing, they are able to feel confident about the details of their video prior to submitting the order. Once they submit the order, they simply print out a shipping label and send us their product. During this waiting period, they are never in the dark- the online order tracker updates them at each step of the production process. Once the first cut of the edit is complete, the user can review the video in browser and easily request edits using time-stamped comments while watching the video. Once the user is satisfied with the video, they download link to their video in web and social-media-ready formats, as well as all raw footage.

Market Research -

We analyzed over 30 reports on eMarketer in order to better understand trends in commercial video. We also conducted a popular media scan, examining news articles, blogs, and press releases to gain insight into current opinions on commercial video and how commercial video needs may evolve.Topics covered in these reports included: influence of video according to industry (click-through, engagement, customer conversion), advertising budgets by industry, challenges of video advertising, use of video in eCommerce and social media, and influence of video on SEO. Understanding trends in commercial video led us to focus sepcifically on improving the video production experience for live action demonstrations of physical products. This is because: following the increase in eCommerce, there is corresponding increase in the demand for product videos and the demonstrated effect that live action product demos, specifically, have on customer conversion. In terms of market size, currently, there are 7,149 film and video production companies in the US alone, generating $43B in revenue annually. Of course, this includes the major production companies that make up the US film industry, which clearly accounts for the majority of revenue. On its own, the online advertising subset of the video market is valued around $5.4B. The statistics around online video, eCommerce, and video advertising in general are promising, with a high level of current activity and predicted growth.


Competitive Analysis -

We conducted a competitive analysis to understand: (1) existing solutions for live action product videos and (2) innovative or stream lined approaches for non-product videos that may be applicable. A survey of 15 video production company websites revealed strong similarities in their advertised process. These companies tend to treat each video project as a unique idea, with a creative process that can last for weeks and cost anywhere from a few thousand dollars to several million. While certain videos may require a full creative process (e.g., comedic or cinematic tv commercials), many videos designed for eCommerce and social media ("Insider" style product demos, "Tasty" style aerial food recipe videos) are strikingly similar in style and content, presenting the opportunity for a streamlined approach. Overall, there is a lack of clarity and transparency regarding cost, end product, and timeline. The only option for potential clients is to submit a quote request form, which seems incredibly cumbersome for clients who are in the research phase of their project and shopping around for potential producers.


Average video costs typically fall into the range of a few thousand dollars (1-10), with most traditional video product companies claiming that their competitors charge more than they do, without listing specific prices. Standard practice among traditional video production companies is to require project-specific information before providing any form of a price quote. This contributes to an atmosphere of opacity that might discourage inexperienced users. Animation companies are more forthright with their prices, often times including ballpark figures in their online ads. However, the inherent differences between animation and live-action video prevent much competition between types; users are either looking for animation specifically or live action specifically, the two are not competitive or interchangeable. Despite these differences, both animation and live-action producers typically fall into the same range of 1-10 thousand dollars for an average 1-3 minute product video. “Product demo video” is a key term used by both animators and live-action video producers to describe their services. This makes it harder to find either specific service type in a list of search engine results. Furthermore, most live-action video companies offer but do not specialize in product demonstration videos. Therefore a user looking for live-action product demo video production may find it difficult to identify the best choice for their needs, due to the presence of animation, overall market saturation, and indistinguishable competitors.


Expert Interview -

A semi-structured 'expert interview' with the founder of EvanScottMoore.com yielded insight into the lack of transparency provided by production companies as well as pain points in his process.

Sample Key Findings:
1. 5/10 of last year’s non-repeat clients were recruited directly through Evan’s website. People are hand-picking Evan based on work he has already done and given SEO, likely doing a lot of research to find him. The majority of his clients send him a link to a video that he's created that is similar to what they want. This suggests that potential clients find relevant portfolio content compelling and a means to communicate.
2. Evan’s wishes there was a way to provide his clients updates on their projects without direct communication. He regularly receives emails and phone calls just checking up on things, even when the project is going according to schedule.
3.Evan’s clients feel underprepared to have a video made (e.g., they don’t realize all they need for a script is a list of product features). They often feel surprised that it's not that hard and from there on have him make several more videos. Demistyfing the process up front may increase customer conversion and decrease their anxiety about the process.
4. Clients are confused about what is relevant or possible in post production (e.g., they often ask about green screen). He wishes he could make it more clear what is easy and difficult to do, what influences pricing, and what he needs from the client in order to create their video.


Email Case Study -

An analysis of email conversation from a product video project from start-to-finish uncovered extreme inefficiency in communication throughout the video production process. Despite this project being "faster and less involved than usual", communication during the pre-production phase involved 25 emails (in addition to phone conversations) and 3 weeks of back-and-forth. Post-production conversation was similarly lengthy, at 23 emails and 1.5 weeks of back-and-forth. This means the majority of the time was spent on clarifying content as opposed to shooting and editing the video. 15 of these emails were simply confirmation emails (e.g., Evan confirming that he received an asset, client confirming that they are okay with suggested change, etc). This suggests a need for a dedicated space to have discussions with an easy way to indicate confirmation.

Is it possible to create a video using only the info collected through our interface? -

Cognitive and market research show that people tend to be happier, less anxious, and more confident making choices when there are fewer options (known as the Paradox of Choice). There is a reason why Squarespace is more user friendly than Wix: Squarespace provides choice within limitation, whereas Wix promotes customizing every pixel. How many of us have spent hours designing our own sneakers on NikeID, only to never actually purchase them, opting instead for their predesigned shoes despite the price similarity? After our collaborative design exercise, we felt that using an interface to 'shop' video features could take the place of an in-person consultation, and that the added transparency could increase users' confidence about what their resulting video would look like. Of course, we were wary of overwhelming users by requiring them to choose every little detail about the video: We wanted users to have just enough control to feel confident about what they were ordering, without burdening them with too much choice.

So, we sought out to test what was the abolute minimal information a video producer needed in order to create the product video the user had in mind. Despite Evan's lengthy consulation meetings and emails with clients as a freelancer, he felt that he only required the following information from them: whether they wanted a solid-color backdrop or studio location, whether there would be actors involved, and what specific features of the product should be demonstrated. I found a video of a product demo online and attempted to have Evan recreate it without him ever watching it. I only provided him with the kinds of details easily attained from an interface (Backdrop color, Voiceover or Actor Speaking on Camera, Captions or No Captions, Duration, and Script). The resulting video was, in our opinion, comparable in style and content, and equivalent or better in quality. Through this prototyping test, we verified that it is indeed feasible to produce high quality product videos using limited instructions communicated solely through an interface. We also realized that we either need to limit the types of products we create videos for, or provide a few more options that are product-specific. For instance, in the video Evan reproduced for this exercise, the original actor wears the product (boots) for a few seconds on camera. If we were to offer apparel videos, we would need to either enable users to specify whether the apparel should be worn and how (e.g., the original actor had pants that covered the upper portion of the boots), or alternatively, make it clear that the apparel will not be showcased on an actual person. Lastly, in this exercise, I provided a verbatim copy of the original script, which many of Evan's regular clients do, but we hypothesized that we could reduce the workload of many clients by having them instead provide a list of product features, and have us generate a draft of a script for them, along with a corresponding shot list that makes it clear what visual will be displayed during each line of the script (e.g., when the script says "This shoe has a flexible sole", a corresponding note might say "Actor uses both hands to slightly bend shoe").

Is this templated, streamlined solution desirable? -

We created a prototype of Video Turbine that is a live, fully functioning WooCommerce website. The decision to use WooCommerce was a tradeoff between control over visual design and functionality, but we ultimately decided while typically, mockups can test the usability and desirability of a product, that the increased functionality and authenticity of a real eCommerce site would help us better assess whether businesses would actually be willing to 'shop' for their video online, the same way they would use an eCommerce site to buy other products. Additionally, to assess desirability, we created an explainer video (see above) for the service which we intend to send around to business owners along with a questionaire. Lastly, throughout the website, we are collecting constant feedback through a Suggestion Box form and a Desired Features poll. So far, we have sent our video and site on to 3 businesses we are connected with and have had valuable feedback, but we will much better be able to evaluate desirability once we put it in front of actual potential customers.


How can we make Video Turbine more usable? -

To find ways to improve the usability of interactions on Video Turbine, we sat down with three adults and had them build a video while thinking aloud and explaining what they thought each step and feature was. They were instructed to 'build a video that looks like this' (we showed them an example video that was not created by Video Turbine). This simple exercise gave us extremely valuable insights both about micro-interactions on the site and copy improvements, but also on desired features.
